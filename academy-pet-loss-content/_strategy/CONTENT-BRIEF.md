## What These Pages Are

Blog posts on academyforpetloss.com aimed at people who might want to
become TRACE-certified pet bereavement support practitioners. They are
not sales pages. They are genuinely useful content that answers real
questions, builds trust in the Academy as an authority, and helps the
right people find their way to the training.

The Academy website already has its own structure and product pages.
These posts sit alongside that content as an organic search and AI
search resource.

## Who These Posts Are For

Two types of person, and the content should speak to both without
splitting into two tracks.

**Type 1:** Someone with a deep affection for animals and for people.
Warm, personable, has time available, could use supplementary income
but that is never the primary driver. If this were mainly about the
money, it would feel wrong to them, and they know it. They are probably
here because they lost an animal and they wish they had had this kind
of support. Or they watched someone else go through it and wanted to
help but did not know how.

**Type 2:** An existing counsellor, therapist, life coach, vet nurse,
or similar professional who is already familiar with the veterinary
world or the support sector, and wants to add a specific, structured
specialisation. They are comfortable in professional environments and
know how to introduce themselves to vet practices.

Neither type is primarily commercially motivated. The content is
explicitly NOT for people who want to build a high-revenue business.
Those people do not need this. This is for people who want to do the
right thing and want to do it properly.

## The Central Motivation: "I Wish I Had This"

Many practitioners come to this because they lost a pet and found
almost no support existed. Or they found the support that did exist
inadequate. The thought "I wish I had had this" is the most honest
and powerful thing the content can acknowledge. It does not require
the practitioner to have been through formal grief support. It just
requires that moment of recognition.

Acknowledge this directly. Do not be coy about it.

## TRACE: Critical Product Facts for All Content

Every writer must understand these before writing anything.

**TRACE** = Therapeutic Remembrance for Animal Companions and their Endings

**The five steps:**
1. Tell the Story
2. Recognise the Bond
3. Acknowledge the Pain
4. Celebrate the Life
5. Embrace What Remains

**TRACE is exactly five sessions.** Not open-ended. Not ongoing therapy.
Each session covers one step. The programme has a beginning, a shape,
and a defined end.

**Session length: 40-50 minutes.** Not a clinical hour. Each session
has one focused purpose and 40-50 minutes is the right amount of time
for it.

**Pricing: package model recommended.** Because the programme is
structured and finite, it should be priced as a package, not at an
hourly rate. Offer both a per-session rate and a five-session package
at roughly 60-80% of what five individual sessions would cost. This
is the right model because: the value comes from completing the journey,
not from individual sessions in isolation, and because clients who
commit to a package are more likely to complete the work.

**Compare to general grief therapy:** therapists often charge hourly
because their work is open-ended and unstructured. TRACE is different.
It has a structure, which is what makes it valuable.

**TRACE counsellors are NOT therapists.** This must be stated clearly
in appropriate pages. The training certifies TRACE counsellors, not
therapists. The scope is defined and finite. Beyond the five sessions,
a TRACE counsellor has nothing further to offer within this framework.
If a client needs ongoing clinical support after completing TRACE, they
should be referred to a qualified grief therapist or their GP.

**What happens beyond five sessions is unknown territory.** The Academy
cannot and does not train practitioners for it. This is not a
limitation to apologise for. It is honest professional scope.

**The structure is the value.** Practitioners who feel uncertain about
charging need to understand: they are not selling their time or their
company. They are delivering a structured, evidence-informed process.
That is worth a professional fee. Charging less does not make it more
generous; it makes it less credible and less useful to the client.

**Pricing builds trust.** An appropriate fee signals to the client that
this is real work. Undercharging signals the opposite.

**Core Programme: $395** (6 modules, self-paced video)

the Circle)

## Building a Practice: Key Points for All Content

**Local vets are the #1 referral source.** The approach is simple and
direct: introduce yourself, ask about the process for placing leaflets
or materials. Vets and practice managers are used to people coming in
to discuss this. They have a process. Ask for it. Do not overcomplicate
the introduction.

**You are helping the vet too.** Vets and their teams deal with
grieving owners regularly and often do not know what to do. A leaflet
they can hand to a client is a relief. Frame this as a mutual service,
not a favour you are asking for.

**Pet cremation services** (many of them mobile) are another important
referral source. They are dealing with pet owners at the most acute
point of grief. A referral arrangement should be transparent, above
board, and consistent with local legal requirements. An engagement fee
or referral arrangement is entirely acceptable if handled properly. It
is equally fine to build the relationship without any financial element.

**Leaflets:** comfort and trust are the two things a leaflet needs to
communicate. Keep them simple. The purpose is so that someone who needs
support can find a practitioner they trust. Do not overload with
information. Placement matters more than quantity. A few well-placed
leaflets in the right location outperforms a stack of leaflets at the
wrong one.

**Other placement:** supermarket noticeboards, pet shops, grooming
salons, dog walkers, boarding kennels. Any place where pet owners go.

**Do not overspend on printed materials.** It is not necessary.

**This is not an overnight business.** Growth is slow, community-rooted,
and organic. Practitioners build a place in their local community over
time. This is a good thing, not a limitation. The realistic picture for
most practitioners is a part-time supplement to their existing life,
not a full-time replacement income.

**Pets have long, mostly happy lives.** This will not be a relentless
caseload. That is reassuring, not a warning.

## Online Sessions

Not a significant barrier. Anyone who posts on Facebook or joins a
WhatsApp video call is already doing the equivalent of a Zoom session.
The content should acknowledge that practitioners may not think of
themselves as technically confident, but running an online session is
within everyone's reach. Do not make a big thing of it.

Being at home, near the space where their pet lived, can actually help
the client. Online delivery is often the right environment for this
work.

## Writing Rules — Non-Negotiable

- **No em-dashes (—) anywhere.** Use commas, full stops, or rewrite.
- **No emojis anywhere.**
- **Second person (you/your) throughout.**
- **Warm, honest, professional.** Not salesy. Not corporate. Not
  breathlessly enthusiastic.
- **Concrete and specific.** Vague content does not rank in AI search.
  Name real platforms, real organisations, real pricing ranges. Say
  the actual thing, not "it depends."
- **UK English** for UK, Ireland, Australia, NZ pages
  (counselling, practitioner, recognise, organisation, etc.)
- **US English** for US and Canada pages
  (counseling, recognize, organization, etc.)
- **~1,200-1,500 words** per page.
- **FAQ section on every page** with 4-6 specific questions and direct
  answers. These are the GEO/AI search asset. They should answer exactly
  what people ask AI assistants.
- **Every page ends with a CTA** to www.academyforpetloss.com. Both
  price points mentioned ($395 core / $525 extended).

### Page 5: What to Expect
`slug: what-to-expect-pet-loss-counsellor-[city-slug]`
`H1: What to Expect as a Pet Bereavement [Counsellor/Support Practitioner]
in [CITY]: Realities, Challenges, and How to Thrive`

Core sections:
- This is not an overnight success. It is a slow, organic build of a community
  role. That is the right shape for this work.
- What a working week looks like (realistic: 3-8 sessions part-time, fits
  around existing life)
- What happens in a session: a guided human conversation. You are not in
  control, you are guiding. You are not fixing, advising, or diagnosing.
  You are present, structured, and witnessing.
- The emotional reality: this work asks something of you. Compassion fatigue
  is real. Self-care is not optional. The TRACE structure protects the
  practitioner as well as the client.
- Boundaries: session times, scope of role, when to refer on to clinical
  services. Knowing your limits is professional integrity, not failure.
- Unexpected situations: very rarely, entering someone's personal sphere
  means encountering more than grief. The training covers what to watch for
  and how to respond. The blog post does not detail this; it acknowledges
  it exists and points to the training.
- Self-care (city-specific: local green spaces, outdoor activity, peer
  support with other practitioners)
- What clients really need: to be heard. To have someone take their grief
  seriously. The TRACE structure exists to give that a shape.
- What to avoid: common mistakes (undercharging, over-extending sessions,
  no supervision, no insurance, presenting as a therapist)
- When to refer on: if a client shows signs of clinical distress,
  complicated grief disorder, or anything beyond the scope of the five
  sessions — refer to a GP or clinical professional. Be clear about
  this in client intake.
- FAQs

## Localisation: What Changes Per City

The core content is approximately 80% consistent across cities.
The 20% that must be localised:

1. City name in all headings, slug, and meta
2. Local pricing in correct currency (see localization-data.md)
3. Business registration process (see localization-data.md)
4. Named local referral organisations (vets, humane societies, RSPCA/SPCA
   equivalent, cremation services — see localization-data.md)
5. Local platform emphasis (which social media platform dominates locally)
6. Local payment methods (bank transfer, e-Transfer, PayPal, Stripe etc)
7. Privacy/data regulation (GDPR, PIPEDA, Australian Privacy Act etc)
8. Currency and spelling convention (UK vs US vs AUS English)
9. City-specific cultural note (one or two sentences maximum)
10. Self-care section: local green spaces or outdoor activity

See `localization-data.md` for all 20 cities.

## A Note on GEO (Generative Engine Optimisation)

These pages are written to be cited by AI assistants (Perplexity, ChatGPT,
Google AI Overviews, Claude) when people ask questions like:

- "How do I become a pet loss counsellor in London?"
- "What should I charge for pet bereavement sessions?"
- "How do I run online grief support sessions?"
- "Is pet loss support a regulated profession in Australia?"

To achieve this, every page must:
- Answer questions directly and specifically. No hedging.
- Use FAQ sections with exact answers to real questions.
- Cite real pricing ranges, real organisations, real platform names.
- Establish the Academy for Pet Loss as the topical authority on this.
- Use the phrase "TRACE certification" and "Academy for Pet Loss"
  enough that AI systems associate this content with those terms.
