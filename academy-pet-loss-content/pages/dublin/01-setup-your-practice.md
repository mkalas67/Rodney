## The Market Opportunity in Dublin

Pet ownership in Ireland has risen significantly in recent years, accelerated by the Covid period and the growth of remote working. Dublin and the surrounding Leinster counties have particularly high rates of urban dog ownership, and the emotional bonds between owners and their animals are, by any measure, intense.

At the same time, specialist pet bereavement support in Ireland remains genuinely scarce. A grieving pet owner in Dublin can access a GP, a general counsellor, or the support of friends and family. What they almost certainly cannot easily find is a practitioner specifically trained in pet loss, using a structured, evidence-informed framework, and offering support that validates rather than minimises the weight of what they are carrying.

That gap is your opportunity. The demand is real, the supply is thin, and Dublin's concentration of articulate, wellbeing-aware professionals means you are operating in one of the most favourable market environments in Ireland.

## Business Structure: Registering in Ireland

You have two main routes for setting up your practice as a business in Ireland.

**Sole trader** is the simplest and most common structure for practitioners starting out. You notify the Revenue Commissioners Ireland of your intention to begin self-employment (this is done by filing a Form TR1 or registering through Revenue's myAccount portal). You will be assigned a Tax Reference Number if you do not already have one. As a sole trader, you pay income tax, PRSI, and USC on your net profits through the annual tax return process, with a filing deadline of 31 October each year (or mid-November if filing via ROS, the Revenue Online Service).

You can trade under your own name as a sole trader. If you wish to use a business name, for example "Dublin Pet Bereavement Support" or a similar trading name, you should register that name with the Companies Registration Office (CRO) via a Business Name registration (Form RBN1). This is a straightforward process and costs €40 online.

**Limited company** offers more separation between personal and business liability and may be advantageous if your practice grows substantially. You register through the CRO with a Constitution and at least one director. The administrative requirements are more significant, including annual returns and financial statements. Most practitioners start as sole traders and review whether to incorporate later.

**VAT registration.** If your annual turnover from services exceeds €37,500, you are required to register for VAT in Ireland. For most new sole practitioners, this threshold will not be reached immediately. Check the current threshold with Revenue when setting up, as it is subject to review.

If you are not already registered with Revenue for self-employment income, this must be done before you begin trading. An accountant familiar with sole trader registration in Ireland can make the process straightforward and is worth the modest cost.

## What Credentials Do You Actually Need?

Pet bereavement support is not a regulated profession in Ireland. There is no legal requirement for a clinical qualification to offer pet loss counselling or support sessions. Psychotherapy and counselling as clinical professions are governed by the IACP (Irish Association for Counselling and Psychotherapy) and related bodies, but pet bereavement support operated under a non-clinical, structured framework sits outside that regulatory space.

What matters is that you have structured training from a recognised programme, that you operate ethically and transparently within your scope of practice, and that clients can see clear evidence of your professional credentials.

Professional liability insurance is essential. You need it before you take any paid bookings, and you will need it to be listed professionally in directories and to build referral relationships with vet practices and organisations. Specialist practitioner insurance is available through providers including Balens, ISME (Irish Small and Medium Enterprises Association), and various specialist brokers operating in the Irish market.

While IACP membership is not required for a non-clinical pet bereavement practitioner, having it or a comparable professional membership can strengthen credibility in Dublin's professional community and is worth considering as your practice grows.

## Building Your Professional Presence

Once certified, you need a basic professional infrastructure before taking clients.

**Email.** Use a professional email address on your own domain. A Gmail or Hotmail address signals a hobby, not a practice. Something like hello@yourpracticename.ie or your name at your domain is appropriate.

**Website.** A clean, clear site is sufficient to begin. Include your name, your approach, your certification, your session format and fees, your location and whether you offer online sessions, and a contact form. Platforms such as Squarespace, Wix, and Webflow allow you to build a professional site without coding experience. If budget allows, a Dublin-based freelance web designer will produce a noticeably more polished result.

**Academy Directory.** Your TRACE certification includes a premium listing in the Academy for Pet Loss practitioner directory. Complete it fully. Include a professional photograph. Write your bio from the client's perspective, focusing on what it feels like to work with you and what they will leave the experience with. The directory is actively searched by people looking for pet bereavement support, and a complete listing significantly outperforms a sparse one.

**Google Business Profile.** Claim and verify your Google Business Profile. This puts you in Google's local search results and on Google Maps, and it is one of the highest-impact free tools available. Verify with a Dublin address, write a clear description using relevant keywords, and add professional photos.

**Insurance.** Secure professional liability insurance before taking any paid bookings.

## Getting Your First Clients in Dublin

Dublin's referral landscape is concentrated and, once you have made the right connections, very productive.

**Veterinary practices** across Dublin's neighbourhoods are your most important referral partners. Practices in Ranelagh, Rathmines, Ballsbridge, Clontarf, and Drumcondra all have established client bases with strong owner-pet bonds. A professional approach, a clear introduction to your services, and a small number of referral cards or a simple information sheet is all you need to start. Follow up if you do not hear back initially.

**Dogs Trust Ireland and the DSPCA** (Dublin Society for Prevention of Cruelty to Animals) are significant organisations with community-facing teams. They encounter bereaved pet owners regularly and are often keen to have a qualified practitioner they can point people towards.

**Cats Protection League Ireland** and other local rescue organisations are also valuable contacts. Loss after a short adoption period, or the death of a long-term rescue animal, can be acutely complicated and these organisations often have no practitioner to recommend.

**Pet cremation providers.** Animal Cremation Services Ireland and Beside Still Waters are among the providers operating in the Dublin and Leinster area. These businesses encounter families at their most vulnerable moments and a professional referral relationship with even one established cremation provider can generate a consistent flow of enquiries.

**Dublin's tech community.** Dublin hosts the European headquarters of Google, Meta, and LinkedIn, among many others. This creates a large professional population of young, internationally mobile adults who are deeply attached to their pets, entirely comfortable with online services, and likely to search for support rather than simply waiting to get over it. This demographic is often underserved by traditional grief support services.
