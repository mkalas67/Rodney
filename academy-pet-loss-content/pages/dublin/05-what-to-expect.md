# What to Expect as a Pet Bereavement Counsellor in Dublin: Realities, Challenges, and How to Thrive

Pet bereavement counselling is meaningful, specific, and emotionally demanding work. Going into it with a realistic understanding of what your working life will look like, what the challenges are, and how to build a practice that sustains you over years rather than months is one of the best investments you can make before you begin. This guide gives you an honest picture of what to expect as a pet bereavement practitioner in Dublin, including the rewards, the harder realities, and the practical habits that distinguish practitioners who thrive from those who burn out.

## Your Typical Working Week

The shape of a pet bereavement practice in Dublin will vary depending on whether you are building part-time alongside other work or moving towards a full-time practice. Here is a realistic picture of what a part-time Dublin practice might look like after six to twelve months of consistent effort.

Monday and Tuesday you might hold five to eight sessions, a mix of clients in ongoing bundles and new enquiries from the week's referrals or directory enquiries. Wednesday becomes your administrative day: responding to new enquiries, sending booking confirmations, completing session notes, and any follow-up with referral partners. Thursday is lighter on sessions, perhaps two to three, with time built in for personal recovery and self-care. Friday you might dedicate to any content or professional development work: a social media post, a brief check-in with a colleague or supervisor, or reviewing your practice's health.

The busiest periods for a Dublin pet bereavement practice tend to cluster around the Christmas and New Year period, when grief of all kinds intensifies. Spring can also bring a surge, as the end of winter and the arrival of lighter days can surface grief that was suppressed during a darker period. Some practitioners find autumn also busy, as the contraction back into home life brings losses to the foreground.

These rhythms are good to anticipate. Building quieter weeks into your calendar during peak periods, by keeping some availability for urgent bookings rather than pre-filling your diary entirely, is a form of professional resilience worth planning for.

## The Emotional Reality: Compassion Fatigue and How TRACE Protects You

Anyone working in grief support is at occupational risk of compassion fatigue. It does not always arrive dramatically. More often it creeps in as a gradual flattening of your response to clients' pain, a growing sense of emotional distance in sessions, difficulty being genuinely present, or a quiet resentment of the work that is deeply at odds with why you chose to do it.

Understanding this risk and having a specific structural protection against it is one of the most important things a pet bereavement practitioner can have. The TRACE framework provides that protection in a way that is built into the method itself, not as an add-on.

Because TRACE gives every session a clear five-step structure (Tell the Story, Recognise the Bond, Acknowledge the Pain, Celebrate the Life, Embrace What Remains), you are never navigating a session without direction. You always know where you are and where you are heading. This structure prevents the kind of session drift that leaves both client and practitioner in a state of unresolved emotional weight at the end of an hour.

The framework's movement towards hope and integration is also significant. TRACE sessions do not end in pain. They end in acknowledgement of what remains: the love, the memories, the changed but continuing relationship. This forward motion means you carry less of the session's emotional weight after the call ends.

These are meaningful protections. They do not replace the need for good self-care habits, peer support, and appropriate session spacing. But they make the work structurally more sustainable than models that leave sessions without a clear ending movement.

## Boundaries: Holding the Line Between Support and Overextension

One of the skills you will develop over time is the ability to be fully present for a client's grief without taking it on as your own. This is a learnable capacity, but it requires attention, particularly in the early months of practice when the work is still new and the emotional pull is strongest.

Your work is to provide a structured, safe, compassionate space in which the client can move through their grief. It is not to fix their grief, to speed it up, to provide constant availability outside sessions, or to become a substitute for the social support they need beyond your sessions.

Clear boundaries around contact between sessions are particularly important. Specify in your initial session what you offer and what you do not: sessions are the container for the work, not ongoing message exchanges. This is not coldness. It is the structure that allows you to show up fully when you are present with a client, without being emotionally available at all times.

Boundaries also mean knowing your scope and being honest when a client needs something you cannot provide. You are trained in pet bereavement support using the TRACE framework. You are not a clinical therapist, a crisis counsellor, or a mental health professional. When a client presents with something that clearly sits outside your scope, your job is to acknowledge what you are seeing, express your concern with genuine care, and clearly direct them towards appropriate support.

## Self-Care in Dublin: Using the City's Spaces

Dublin offers more varied and accessible natural respite than many people who have not explored it give it credit for.

The coastal walks at Sandymount Strand and around Dublin Bay provide something that very few European capital cities can: the capacity to be at the edge of the sea within 20 to 30 minutes of the city centre. After a heavy session day, a walk along the strand, particularly in the early morning or at dusk when it is quieter, delivers a quality of restoration that is difficult to replicate elsewhere.

The Phoenix Park is Europe's largest enclosed urban park and is genuinely underused by Dublin professionals who treat it as a weekend destination rather than an accessible daily resource. A 30-minute walk through the park after an afternoon of sessions can reset your nervous system in a way that staying at your desk cannot.

St Anne's Park in Clontarf, Marlay Park in Rathfarnham, and the canal towpaths from Portobello to Rathmines all offer quieter, walking-pace green spaces that are practical for weekday self-care rather than requiring a special trip.

Dublin also has a growing community of wellbeing practitioners, therapists, and coaches. Finding one or two trusted peers who understand the nature of your work is an investment in your long-term sustainability. Peer support does not replace formal supervision but it provides something equally valuable: the normalisation of the experience of doing this kind of work.

Formal supervision from a practitioner familiar with grief or counselling work is worth establishing even though it is not mandatory for non-clinical practitioners. A monthly supervision session gives you a dedicated space to process complex cases, explore your own reactions, and maintain perspective. Dublin has enough qualified supervisors that finding someone experienced in grief and support work should not be difficult.

## What Dublin Clients Actually Need From You

Dublin clients, particularly those in the city's professional population, often arrive at their first session carrying an extra layer of self-consciousness about the intensity of their grief. Irish culture has made significant strides in openness about mental health and emotional experience, but the loss of a pet can still provoke the internal question: "Am I allowed to feel this bad about this?"

Your most important function before anything else is to answer that question. Yes, this is a real loss. Yes, what you are feeling is a real grief response. No, it does not need to be compared to the loss of a human family member to be valid. The simple act of receiving a client's grief without minimising it, without rushing it, and without framing it as disproportionate is often the most therapeutic thing you do in an entire session.

The TRACE framework begins with Tell the Story for precisely this reason. Before any therapeutic processing can occur, the client needs to feel believed. When someone is genuinely, fully heard in the story of their animal and their loss, without interruption or judgement, the quality of their engagement with the rest of the session changes. They can exhale. The work can begin.

Irish warmth, a genuine asset in your work, is a quality you likely already have. The TRACE framework gives it structure. Together they are a formidable combination.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Underpricing your practice from the start.** Dublin is not a low-cost market. Professional services command professional rates, and pricing too low does not attract more clients. It signals lower confidence in your own work. Price at the lower end of the market range while new, but start within the professional range.

**Taking on every client regardless of fit.** Not every person who contacts you will be right for what you offer. Someone presenting with acute clinical depression alongside pet loss needs a GP referral alongside or instead of your support. Someone in crisis needs crisis services. Knowing this and acting on it is not a failure. It is professional practice.

**Skipping session notes.** Write brief notes after every session, even if they are only a few lines. Notes help you track each client's progress across sessions, ensure you remember important details before the next session, and provide a professional record if you ever need to demonstrate what you covered with a client.

**Isolating professionally.** Dublin has a real community of practitioners in related fields. Attending events, joining professional networks, and being visible in the local wellbeing community keeps you connected, generates referrals, and prevents the isolation that affects many sole practitioners.

## When to Refer to Clinical Services

The TRACE framework is designed for pet bereavement support. It is not designed for clinical mental health intervention. There will be clients whose presentations clearly indicate that clinical support is needed alongside or instead of what you offer.

Refer to a GP or clinical mental health service when you observe: expressions of suicidal ideation or intent; signs of severe, debilitating depression that is preventing daily functioning; psychotic symptoms; active substance misuse or dependence; or a history of serious mental health conditions presenting as current and acute.

When referring, do so with warmth and clarity. You are not rejecting the client. You are advocating for them to receive the level of support their situation requires. A list of Dublin GP practices, community mental health services through the HSE, and the Samaritans Ireland (116 123) should be kept close to hand.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Will the work become emotionally easier over time?**
Most practitioners find that it becomes more manageable over time, rather than easier in the sense of being less emotionally present. What changes is that you develop greater capacity to hold the work without it overflowing into the rest of your life. Good habits, peer support, and a structured framework make the development of that capacity significantly faster.

**How do I handle a client whose grief seems disproportionate even to me?**
Grief responses vary enormously and are shaped by the nature of the relationship, by previous losses, and by many other factors not always visible in a first session. Your job is not to assess whether a client's grief is proportionate. It is to be present with what they bring and to use the TRACE framework to help them move through it. If the presentation raises genuine clinical concerns, refer on.

**Is there a growing market for pet bereavement support in Dublin specifically?**
Yes. Pet ownership in Ireland has grown significantly and is highest in urban, professional demographics. Dublin's population of young professionals, many working in the tech sector, has high rates of pet ownership and high comfort with seeking specialist support. The market exists and is underserved.

**How do I stay motivated during slow periods when the diary is quiet?**
Slow periods are normal in any professional practice, particularly in the first year. Use them for professional development, marketing, content creation, and relationship building rather than treating them as evidence that the practice is not working. The practitioners who push through quiet patches and keep showing up consistently are the ones with full diaries twelve months later.

**Can I specialise further within pet bereavement practice?**
Yes. The Extended Programme at the Academy for Pet Loss includes Complex Loss and Widening the Circle modules that allow you to develop specific expertise in the more complicated presentations you will encounter. Additional training in grief theory, trauma, or complementary approaches can further deepen your practice over time.
