# How to Run Online Pet Loss Support Sessions in Manchester

Online sessions are not an inferior version of face-to-face support. For many pet loss clients, they are the better option. For practitioners in Manchester, online delivery removes the cost of room hire, extends your geographic reach across Greater Manchester and beyond, and gives you the flexibility to build a practice that fits around your existing commitments. This guide covers everything you need to run professional online pet loss sessions from the outset.

---

## Why Online Works Especially Well for Pet Loss Support

Pet bereavement is one of the therapeutic contexts where online delivery offers a genuine advantage, not simply a logistical convenience.

The home is where a client's relationship with their pet was lived. The chair where the cat slept, the garden where the dog ran, the corner where the rabbit's hutch stood. These spaces hold the grief in a way that a neutral consulting room cannot. When clients are in their own home during a session, they are often more emotionally accessible, more willing to reach for a photograph or a favourite toy, and more naturally connected to the memories you are working to honour together.

There is also a practical barrier that online sessions remove. In the days immediately after a pet dies, many clients are in acute grief and find it genuinely difficult to travel. The period when they most need support is also the period when leaving the house feels most impossible. Online sessions meet them where they are.

For practitioners in Manchester, online delivery offers a geographic advantage. From a single broadband connection, you can work with clients across Greater Manchester, across the North West, and across the UK. For a field with very few qualified practitioners, being reachable to clients in Bury, Oldham, Stockport, or rural Cheshire without any logistical overhead is a real competitive advantage during your practice-building phase.

## Which Platform Should You Use?

For most practitioners in Manchester starting out, Zoom is the best choice and the industry standard.

Zoom is familiar to the large majority of clients, works reliably across a range of devices and connection speeds, and provides the features you need for professional sessions: a waiting room that allows you to admit clients individually, password-protected meetings, end-to-end encryption, and the option to record sessions with consent. A basic paid Zoom plan costs approximately £12 to £14 per month. The free plan limits sessions to 40 minutes, which is unsuitable for counselling sessions.

Google Meet is free and fully functional for one-to-one video calls. It integrates well if you use Google Workspace for email and calendar management. The trade-off is that it lacks some professional-feel features, such as a waiting room by default. If your clients are comfortable with it and you are in the early stages of practice, it is a workable no-cost option.

Microsoft Teams is known to most clients through their workplace, which can feel either reassuring or overly formal depending on the client. It is not the most intuitive choice for a grief support context and is best used only when a client specifically requests it.

Doxy.me is a telehealth platform designed specifically for practitioners. No app download is required: clients simply click a link and enter a virtual waiting room. It is HIPAA-compliant, has a clinical feel without being cold, and is widely used by UK counsellors who want a platform built from the ground up for this purpose. A free tier exists with basic features; paid plans add functionality. It is worth considering if you prefer a more dedicated professional tool.

Simple Practice is a full practice management suite that includes video sessions alongside scheduling, billing, intake forms, and client records. The cost is higher (typically $69 or more per month USD) and the platform is best suited to established practitioners running a full caseload who want everything integrated in one system.

Recommended starting combination for Manchester practitioners: Zoom for sessions, Calendly or Acuity for scheduling, and Stripe or direct bank transfer for payment.

## Setting Up Your Online Space

Your online environment forms part of your professional impression. Clients assess your credibility partly from the quality of your setup, and it is worth taking fifteen minutes to get it right.

Lighting is the most impactful element. Natural light from a window facing you is ideal. If there is a window behind you, you will appear as a dark silhouette. A simple desk lamp or ring light placed in front of you is a low-cost solution. Test your lighting on a practice call before your first client session.

Audio quality matters more than most people assume. In Greater Manchester, where many practitioners work from homes in dense residential areas, background noise is a common problem. A USB microphone or a quality headset with a built-in microphone improves audio clarity significantly and is worth the investment. Clients in grief need to hear you without straining.

Your background should be simple and warm. A plain wall, a bookshelf, or an uncluttered corner of a room works well. Avoid virtual backgrounds for professional sessions: they pixelate around your face and create a visual restlessness that is distracting in a grief context.

Camera angle should be approximately at eye level. Raising your laptop on a stand or a stack of books is usually sufficient. A low-angled camera looking up at you is unflattering and conveys a sense of informality that is inappropriate for professional client work.

Run a full technical check before your first session: camera, microphone, internet connection, and platform functionality. Do not discover a problem at the start of a session with a grieving client.

## Managing the Client Experience Remotely

The client experience of your online practice begins before the session starts. Your confirmation email should include the meeting link, session time, brief instructions for accessing the platform, and a suggestion to find a quiet, private space for the call. In Manchester households, which are often busy, a note about the value of privacy is more than a formality.

A brief opening ritual settles clients into the session. This does not need to be elaborate: a short check-in, a moment of grounding, a simple question about how they are arriving today. It marks the boundary between the ordinary day and the focused space of the session and is particularly important in online delivery, where the transition from everyday life to session is less physical than it would be walking into a consulting room.

The first step of the TRACE framework, Tell the Story, works naturally in an online context. Clients can share photographs through the camera, hold up a treasured object, or describe the physical spaces in their home where their pet lived. This kind of detail would be less accessible in a neutral consulting room.

Build a closing ritual: a five-minute wind-down in which you summarise what was covered, acknowledge what the client is carrying, and confirm the next session if there is one. Ending a session abruptly because a timer has gone off is jarring in any therapeutic context.

Send a brief follow-up message after each session, particularly in the early phase of working with a client. Two or three sentences acknowledging the session and confirming next contact is sufficient. It is a small action that makes a significant difference to how supported the client feels between sessions.

## Scheduling and Payment Tools

Calendly and Acuity Scheduling are both well suited to a solo practice and integrate directly with Zoom. Calendly is simpler and its free plan covers basic scheduling. Acuity has additional features including intake forms and automatic session reminders, which are particularly useful when working with clients who may be managing grief-related concentration difficulties. Both allow you to set your availability, share a booking link, and receive automatic confirmations.

For payments, the most practical options for UK practitioners are Stripe (integrates with Calendly and Acuity, processes card payments automatically), direct bank transfer (zero transaction fees but requires manual tracking and confirmation), and PayPal (familiar to clients but slightly more friction than Stripe). Most practitioners in Manchester collect payment at the time of booking to avoid chasing invoices.

Make booking as frictionless as possible. A direct calendar and payment link in your email signature, on your Academy directory listing, and on any social profile or website means a client can go from finding you to having a session confirmed in under five minutes. Every additional step between decision and booking is a point where a grieving client may not follow through.

## Privacy, Confidentiality and Data Protection

As a UK practitioner, you operate under the UK GDPR framework. This requires you to be able to explain to clients what personal data you hold about them, why you hold it, how it is stored, and how long you keep it. A short, plain-language privacy notice is sufficient and should be included in your client intake documents.

Client notes should be stored securely. An encrypted cloud drive (such as Google Drive with two-factor authentication, or a dedicated encrypted service) or a password-protected file is appropriate. Do not store client notes in an unprotected notes app or an ordinary email inbox.

Use password-protected meeting links for all sessions. On Zoom, enable the waiting room so that you admit each client individually, preventing any possibility of an uninvited attendee joining. Never record a session without the client's explicit prior written consent. In your consent form, explain clearly how any recording would be stored and when it would be deleted.

Conduct sessions in a private space where you will not be overheard. In shared Manchester households, this sometimes requires advance planning.

Your client intake form should include a data protection statement confirming what personal data you hold and the basis on which you hold it. It does not need to be lengthy, but it needs to be present.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does running online sessions in Manchester limit my ability to build community referrals?
No. Your referral relationships with local vets, Manchester Dogs' Home, and cremation services are built in person or by letter, regardless of whether your sessions are online or in-person. The session format is separate from the referral relationship. Many practitioners with fully online practices have strong local referral networks.

### What do I do if a client in acute distress wants to end a session early?
Gently affirm that they can take a break at any time. If a client is in crisis, do not end the session until you have a clear safety plan: confirm they have a person to contact, share the Samaritans number (116 123), and where necessary encourage them to contact their GP. Your TRACE training covers how to manage acute distress within a session.

### Can I run group sessions online for Manchester-based clients?
Yes, and this can be an effective and financially efficient extension of your one-to-one practice. A small group of four to six bereaved pet owners meeting online weekly or fortnightly can create a powerful peer support element alongside your facilitation. This is typically a more advanced offering, introduced once your individual practice is established, but it is a natural extension of the TRACE framework.

### What is the best time of day to offer online pet loss sessions in Manchester?
Early mornings before the working day, lunchtimes, and evenings from 6pm to 8pm are the most popular slots for clients who are in employment. Weekend morning sessions attract a different demographic, often those with older animals who have more flexible schedules. Offering a mix across the week gives you the broadest reach.

### How do I introduce the TRACE framework to a client at the start of their first session?
Simply and honestly. "The approach I use is called TRACE, which stands for Tell the Story, Recognise the Bond, Acknowledge the Pain, Celebrate the Life, and Embrace What Remains. We will move through these steps at your pace, starting wherever feels right for you." Most clients find this framing reassuring because it gives shape to something that currently feels shapeless.
