# How to Run Online Pet Loss Support Sessions in Austin

Online sessions are not a compromise or a second-best option for pet bereavement support. For most clients in Austin, they are the preferred format. This page covers why online works so well for this kind of work, which platforms to use, how to set up your tech environment, and how to create a client experience that feels professional, warm, and safe from the first moment of contact.

---

## Why Online Sessions Work Especially Well for Pet Loss

Grief is private. When a pet dies, many people feel embarrassed by the intensity of their feelings, partly because they have absorbed the cultural message that losing an animal should not hurt as much as it does. Reaching out for support takes courage. Coming to a stranger's office, or having a stranger come to their home, adds friction that can prevent someone from seeking help at all.

Online sessions lower that barrier significantly. A grieving client in Austin can join a session from their own sofa, with the pet's blanket on their lap if they want it. They can cry without feeling exposed. They can end the session and take a moment in a familiar space. The intimacy and safety of their own environment is actually a clinical advantage for this type of work, not a limitation.

Austin is also a digital-first city. The professionals who make up a large portion of your potential client base work remotely, use video calls daily, and are entirely comfortable with Zoom-based services. Online sessions are not unusual here. They are normal.

Finally, online sessions allow you to serve clients across all of Austin's neighbourhoods, from South Congress to Pflugerville, without anyone facing a long drive across town. That geographic reach matters in a city where traffic is a real deterrent.

## Platform Comparison

Several video platforms work for online sessions. These are the main ones practitioners use.

**Zoom (recommended):** Zoom is the most widely trusted video platform for professional conversations in the US. Most clients already have it installed or can set it up in under five minutes. The interface is familiar, stable, and reliable. The paid Personal Pro plan (approximately $14.99 per month) removes the 40-minute session limit for free accounts and gives you dedicated meeting rooms with consistent links. Zoom also offers waiting rooms, session recording (useful only if the client has explicitly consented), and breakout rooms if you run group sessions. For most pet loss practitioners in Austin, Zoom is the clear first choice.

**Google Meet:** Free, simple, and widely used. No account required for clients. A good backup option, and worth knowing if a client cannot use Zoom. Slightly less polished for professional use but perfectly functional.

**Doxy.me:** Designed specifically for health and wellness practitioners. It has a simple waiting room interface and markets itself on its HIPAA-aligned design. Worth considering if you work alongside clinical practitioners or want an interface that signals healthcare-adjacent professionalism.

**Teams (Microsoft) or FaceTime:** Not recommended as primary platforms. Teams is corporate-oriented and adds friction. FaceTime is Apple-only and too informal for professional sessions.

## Tech Setup

Your tech environment directly affects how your sessions feel to clients. It does not need to be expensive, but it should be intentional.

**Camera:** The built-in camera on most modern laptops is adequate. A dedicated webcam (Logitech C920 or similar, around $80) improves image quality and allows you to position the camera at eye level more easily, which reads as more direct and connected.

**Microphone:** Clear audio is more important than sharp video. A USB condenser microphone or a quality headset removes background noise and makes your voice crisp. An AirPods or similar earbud setup is acceptable if audio quality is good.

**Lighting:** Sit facing a window or use a softbox lamp facing you. Backlighting (a window behind you) puts your face in shadow and makes the session feel less connected. Good lighting makes you visible, warm, and present.

**Background:** Keep it clean and calm. A plain wall, a simple bookshelf, or a Zoom virtual background that is neutral and professional all work. Avoid distracting clutter. If you have a quiet room, use it.

**Internet connection:** Ethernet is more reliable than WiFi. If you are using WiFi, run a speed test (fast.com) before you begin. You want at least 10 Mbps upload for smooth video.

**Headset or headphones:** Using headphones prevents audio echo and is strongly recommended. Your client should hear only your voice, not their own words echoing back to them.

## Creating a Great Client Experience

The technical setup matters, but the experience your client has from the first contact to the final session is what they will remember and recommend.

**Intake and preparation:** Send a brief intake form before the first session. Ask for the pet's name, species, and the circumstances of the loss. Knowing these details before you begin means the client does not have to reintroduce their loss from scratch, and it signals that you have prepared for them specifically.

**Before the session:** Send a confirmation email with the Zoom link, a rough outline of how the session will flow, and a note about what the client might want to have to hand (a photograph of their pet, a favourite object, a glass of water). This reduces first-session anxiety and builds anticipation in a good way.

**Session opening:** Do not rush into the TRACE framework immediately. Allow a minute or two for the client to settle. A simple "How are you feeling as you come into this today?" acknowledges that joining a video call in the middle of grief takes something from them.

**During the session:** Periodically check that the client is comfortable. Online sessions can be more tiring than in-person work because the client has to focus on a screen. Build in natural pauses. Normalise silence.

**After the session:** Send a brief follow-up email within 24 hours. Acknowledge what was shared, offer one or two brief reflections or resources if appropriate, and confirm the next session if booked. This costs five minutes and meaningfully extends the value of each session.

## Scheduling and Payment

A smooth booking and payment process is part of the professional experience.

**Calendly:** The standard tool for session scheduling. Clients click a link, see your available times, and book without a back-and-forth email exchange. Set up your calendar blocks in advance and let Calendly send automated reminders 24 hours and one hour before each session. Austin clients, accustomed to app-based booking for everything from restaurants to personal trainers, will appreciate not having to call or email to schedule.

**Payment:** Stripe is the most professional and widely trusted option. It integrates with Calendly to allow upfront payment at the time of booking, which eliminates no-shows and reduces admin. Square and PayPal are also solid alternatives. Avoid cash-only or invoice-only arrangements early in your practice, as they create unnecessary friction.

**Cancellation policy:** State a clear policy upfront. A 24 or 48-hour cancellation window is standard. Clients who understand the policy before booking are less likely to dispute it.

## US Confidentiality Considerations

Online sessions do not change your core ethical obligations, but they introduce some specific points worth noting.

You are not a licensed mental health practitioner, which means HIPAA's strict requirements do not apply to your work in the same way they would to a therapist. However, maintaining confidentiality is both an ethical obligation and a foundational trust issue.

Use a secure platform (Zoom, Meet, or Doxy.me). Do not record sessions without explicit written consent. Store any notes or intake forms securely, preferably in a password-protected document or simple practice management tool. Do not discuss client details on social media or with third parties. These practices protect your clients and your reputation.

If you are ever uncertain about whether you should refer a client to a licensed mental health professional, err toward referral. Providing the client with a list of licensed counselors or therapists in Austin alongside your own support is never wrong.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I see clients in other US states through online sessions?**
Yes. Because pet loss support is not a regulated clinical service, you are not restricted by state licensure in the way a therapist would be. You can serve clients across the US.

**What if a client's internet connection is unstable?**
Have a phone number ready as a backup. If video drops, switch to an audio call. Let clients know at the start of your first session that you have a backup plan, which removes anxiety about technical problems.

**Do clients need to set up a Zoom account?**
No. Clients can join a Zoom meeting via a browser link without an account. This makes access simple, especially for clients who are not technically confident.

**How long should an online session be?**
50-60 minutes is the standard. For a first session, 60 minutes gives enough time for intake, the TRACE framework, and closure. Follow-up sessions of 50 minutes are common.

**Should I allow clients to use a phone rather than a computer for sessions?**
Video is preferred because the visual connection matters. But if a client is in difficult circumstances, an audio-only session is better than no session. Be flexible in your intake process and let clients tell you what works for them.
