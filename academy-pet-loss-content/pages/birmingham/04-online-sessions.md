# How to Run Online Pet Loss Support Sessions in Birmingham

Online delivery is not a compromise for Birmingham pet bereavement practitioners; it is an advantage. This guide covers everything you need to run professional, effective online sessions: the right platform, your physical setup, managing the client experience, tools for scheduling and payment, and your legal obligations around data.

---

## Why Online Works Particularly Well for Pet Loss Support

Pet loss work has a quality that makes online sessions more effective than in many other forms of support: the client is most comfortable in the space they shared with their animal.

When a client sits in the room where their dog used to sleep, or near the window their cat favoured, or with the collar still on the side table, they are surrounded by the textures of the relationship they are grieving. This is not a distraction; it is exactly the material that the TRACE framework is designed to work with. Tell the Story, Recognise the Bond, and Celebrate the Life are all richer when the client is in their own environment.

For Birmingham specifically, online delivery removes significant practical barriers. The city spreads across a large geographic area. Someone in Sutton Coldfield may be 45 minutes from a consulting room in Edgbaston. Someone in Solihull may have difficulty parking, or limited mobility, or caring responsibilities that make leaving the house genuinely difficult. Online sessions mean you can serve the entire Birmingham travel-to-work area and beyond without either party travelling.

You also, practically, do not need to rent a consulting room. This keeps your overheads low, particularly in the early months of building your caseload.

## Choosing a Platform: An Honest Comparison

**Zoom** is the best choice for most pet bereavement practitioners. It is widely familiar to clients, reliable on both desktop and mobile, easy to set up, and has strong security features when configured correctly. The free tier limits meetings to 40 minutes; the Pro plan (currently around £12/month) removes this limit and adds recording capability and a waiting room. Recommended for almost all practitioners.

**Google Meet** is free and requires no installation on the client side (they open it in Chrome). It is slightly less stable than Zoom on slower broadband connections and has fewer settings, but it is perfectly adequate. It is a good option if your clients tend to be less technical and you want to minimise setup friction for them.

**Microsoft Teams** is familiar in corporate environments. If you are offering pet loss support as part of a workplace programme or partnering with Birmingham-based businesses, Teams may be worth using because clients already have it open. For a private practice, it is more complex to set up than necessary.

**Doxy.me** is a healthcare-focused video platform built with clinical practitioners in mind. It runs in a browser (no download needed), has a virtual waiting room, and stores no session data. It is GDPR-compliant by design. It is a reasonable choice if you prefer a clinical-grade platform or if you plan to work alongside NHS or private healthcare referral sources in Birmingham. The free tier is functional; the Pro tier costs around $35/month.

For a Birmingham private practice, the recommendation is: **Zoom Pro for most practitioners**, Google Meet as a simple free alternative, Doxy.me if you are building clinical referral relationships.

## Setting Up Your Space

The quality of your physical environment communicates a great deal before you say a word. Clients in distress are acutely sensitive to atmosphere.

**Lighting.** Natural light from a window is ideal, but place the window in front of you (facing you) not behind you. A window behind you turns you into a silhouette. If your setup does not allow front-facing natural light, buy a ring light or a small LED panel (£20-£40 on Amazon). Good lighting is one of the highest-return setup investments you can make.

**Audio.** Your built-in laptop microphone is usually adequate for most clients on broadband, but a USB headset (such as a Jabra or Logitech model, around £40-£60) removes all ambient noise and guarantees consistent audio quality. Poor audio is the most common cause of client fatigue on long calls.

**Background.** A plain wall, a neat bookshelf, or a simple indoor plant are all appropriate. Avoid cluttered backgrounds, anything that might distract the client, and virtual backgrounds (they shift and glitch in ways that are subtly disorienting). Your background should be calm and professional.

**Privacy.** Ensure the room cannot be entered during a session. Tell household members your schedule. Silence your phone completely (not vibrate). Closing the door is the minimum; a brief note on the outside of the door is good practice if others are regularly in the house.

**Your own body.** Sit slightly back from the camera so your face and upper body are both visible. Do not look down at your notes; if you need to refer to something, tell the client you are briefly glancing at your notes rather than going silent and breaking eye contact.

## Managing the Client Experience

The session itself is only part of the work. How you manage the experience around it shapes whether a client returns, refers others, or leaves feeling held.

**Confirmation emails.** Send an automated confirmation as soon as a session is booked. Include the date, time, the session link (Zoom or equivalent), a brief note about what to have ready (a glass of water, tissues if needed, no obligations), and your cancellation policy. This email does a lot of emotional work: it reassures the client they made the right decision by booking.

**Session rituals.** The TRACE framework opens each session with Tell the Story, but you can create a brief transition ritual before that. A few seconds of grounding: "Take a moment to settle. There is no rush here." This signals to the client that the session is different from the rest of their day and that they have your full attention.

**Closing.** Do not end abruptly. Summarise what you noticed: "Today you told me about the way he used to wait at the door. I want you to hold that image with you this week." Give the client a moment before they leave the call. A warm, unhurried close is disproportionately memorable.

**Follow-up.** A brief, warm email within 24 hours of each session ("Thank you for sharing with me today. I am here when you are ready for our next time together") takes two minutes and significantly increases the likelihood of rebooking. Do not include clinical content in a follow-up email; keep it brief and human.

## Scheduling and Payment Tools

**Calendly** (calendly.com) is the simplest scheduling tool for solo practitioners. You set your availability, share your Calendly link, and clients book directly into your calendar. The free tier is sufficient for a single session type. The paid tier (around £10/month) allows multiple session types (single, package, free introductory call) and integrates with payment processors.

**Payment.** The cleanest options for UK practitioners are:

- **Stripe** integrates directly with Calendly and processes card payments automatically at the time of booking. There is no monthly fee; Stripe charges approximately 1.5% + 20p per transaction.
- **PayPal** is widely recognised by clients and requires no technical setup beyond a business account. Fees are slightly higher than Stripe (around 2.9% + 30p), but some clients prefer it.
- **Bank transfer (BACS)** is free and simple. State your bank details in your confirmation email and invoice. It requires you to manually check payments before sessions, which adds admin time but costs nothing.

For an early-career Birmingham practice, bank transfer with Calendly for scheduling is a workable no-cost combination. Add Stripe once you have five or more clients per week.

## GDPR and Data Protection

As a practitioner holding client information (names, contact details, notes about personal grief), you have legal obligations under UK GDPR.

The essentials:

- Register with the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) as a data controller. Annual fee is £40 for most small practitioners. Do this before you take a first client.
- Use a privacy policy on your website (free templates available from the ICO website; customise them for your practice).
- Do not record sessions without explicit, documented consent from the client. If you do record (for supervision purposes), store recordings securely and delete them promptly.
- Store client notes on an encrypted device or a cloud service with UK data processing (Google Workspace, Microsoft OneDrive with UK data residency). Do not keep notes on an unsecured email account or plain text on a shared computer.
- Have a clear data retention policy: how long do you keep notes after a client relationship ends? Two years is a common standard for non-clinical practitioners.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What happens if the video call drops during a session?**
Agree this in advance with clients: if the call drops, wait two minutes, then both parties attempt to reconnect via the same link. If reconnection fails, continue by phone until the session is complete.

**Can I run online sessions from a cafe or co-working space in Birmingham?**
No. You cannot guarantee client confidentiality in a public space. Your session location must always be private.

**Do I need to offer in-person sessions as well?**
No. Many practitioners in Birmingham run entirely online practices. There is no regulatory requirement for in-person work, and client demand for online sessions is strong.

**What if a client becomes very distressed during a session?**
This is addressed in your TRACE training. Have your local crisis contact numbers (Birmingham MIND: 0121 262 3555, Samaritans: 116 123) readily accessible. Your role is compassionate support; if a client is in acute crisis, refer to appropriate services.

**Is online pet loss support covered by my insurance?**
Confirm this with your insurer (Balens or Towergate). Most policies explicitly cover online delivery; specify this when you take out the policy.
