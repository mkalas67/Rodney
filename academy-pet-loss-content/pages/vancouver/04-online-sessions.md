# How to Run Online Pet Loss Counselling Sessions in Vancouver

Online sessions are not a compromise version of in-person support. For pet bereavement counselling specifically, they are often the better option. Clients who have just lost an animal are frequently unable to leave home, especially in the first days of grief. The ability to sit in their own space, perhaps with a surviving pet beside them or near their animal's favourite spot, can make the conversation richer and more honest than anything that happens across a desk in a professional office.

In Vancouver, with its tech-forward culture and geographically spread population (from the North Shore to Surrey to Langley), online delivery also makes your practice accessible to the entire Metro Vancouver area and beyond, without the cost or inconvenience of a physical premises.

This page covers everything you need to deliver online sessions professionally and safely: platform options, technical setup, client experience, payment and scheduling, and privacy obligations under Canadian law.

---

## Why Online Suits Pet Loss Counselling

Pet loss is unique among grief experiences in several ways that make online delivery particularly well-suited to it.

First, the grief often hits suddenly. Euthanasia decisions, unexpected illness, accidents: many clients are not in a state to travel when they first need support. Being able to book a session and receive it from home reduces the barrier to getting help at the right time.

Second, many clients want to be surrounded by their animal's things: the favourite bed, the collar hanging on the hook, the photo on the wall. These physical reminders are comforting during a TRACE session, not distracting. An online format makes that possible.

Third, Vancouver is a geographically diverse metro area. Someone in Deep Cove or White Rock is unlikely to travel to a specific office for a single appointment. Online sessions mean you can serve the whole region without either of you compromising on access.

Fourth, Vancouver clients are digitally literate. The city's strong tech and startup culture means video calls for professional services are entirely normal. You will rarely encounter a client who does not know how to use Zoom.

## Platform Comparison

**Zoom (recommended).** Zoom is the most widely used and most trusted video platform for professional services in Canada. Your clients almost certainly already have it installed. The free tier allows one-to-one sessions of unlimited length. The Pro plan (approximately USD $15 per month) adds useful features like cloud recording and custom waiting rooms. Zoom's security settings (waiting rooms, session passcodes) are sufficient for confidential counselling conversations. This is the right choice for most practitioners.

**Google Meet.** Free, integrated with Gmail, and requires no software download for clients using a browser. A reasonable backup option but less familiar in a professional services context than Zoom.

**Doxy.me.** Designed specifically for healthcare and counselling providers. It runs in a browser, requires no account from the client, and is HIPAA-compliant. Some counsellors prefer it for its privacy positioning. It is less familiar to clients, which can create a small barrier.

**Microsoft Teams.** Suitable if you are already operating in a Microsoft business environment. Not recommended as a primary platform for a solo wellness practice due to its corporate associations.

**What to avoid.** WhatsApp and FaceTime are not appropriate for professional sessions. They lack waiting rooms, professional branding, and adequate session management features.

## Technical Setup

Your setup does not need to be expensive, but it does need to be reliable and professional.

**Internet.** A stable wired connection is better than Wi-Fi for sessions. If you are using Wi-Fi, ensure your router is nearby and that you are not competing with other heavy internet users in the household during session times.

**Camera.** The camera built into most modern laptops is adequate. If your laptop camera is more than five years old, a USB webcam in the CAD $50 to $100 range will give noticeably better image quality.

**Lighting.** This matters more than most new practitioners realise. Face a window or use a simple ring light (available for CAD $30 to $60) so that your face is clearly lit. Do not sit with a bright window behind you.

**Microphone.** A USB headset or desktop microphone improves audio clarity significantly over a built-in laptop microphone. The Blue Snowball or Yeti (available at Best Buy or London Drugs in Vancouver for CAD $60 to $130) are popular choices.

**Background.** Use a tidy, neutral background. A bookshelf, a plain wall, or a tasteful plant work well. If your home background is distracting, use a simple virtual background in Zoom, though a real background usually reads as warmer.

**Quiet space.** Book sessions during times when your home is quiet. Inform anyone else in the household that you are in a session. Consider a "do not disturb" sign on your door.

## Client Experience

The quality of the client's experience starts before the session begins.

**Booking confirmation.** Send a confirmation email that includes the Zoom link, a brief reminder of what to expect, and a suggestion to find a quiet private space with a box of tissues nearby. This small act of preparation signals care and professionalism.

**Technology check.** For new clients, offer a brief five-minute technology check the day before their first session. This prevents the first five minutes of a session being consumed by login problems.

**The session itself.** Start with a warm check-in. Ask how the client is doing right now, before you move into the TRACE structure. Acknowledge that speaking over video about something this painful takes courage. The five TRACE steps provide the framework, but your warmth and presence provide the container.

**Session notes.** Take brief notes during or immediately after sessions. These help you track progress across a package and ensure continuity between sessions. Store notes securely (see Privacy section below).

**Follow-up.** A brief follow-up email after the first session, acknowledging what was covered and what comes next, reinforces the professional relationship and demonstrates care.

## Scheduling and Payment in Vancouver

**Scheduling tools.** Calendly (free tier) or Acuity Scheduling (CAD $20 to $25 per month) allow clients to book sessions directly into your calendar without back-and-forth emails. Both integrate with Zoom to automatically generate session links. This is highly recommended as it saves significant administrative time.

**Payment methods.** In Canada, e-Transfer (Interac e-Transfer) is the standard for paying for personal services. It is fast, familiar to all Canadian bank customers, and free or low-cost through most bank accounts. Your clients will expect to use it. Set up e-Transfer acceptance through your bank and include your e-Transfer email address in your payment instructions.

Stripe and PayPal are both effective for clients who prefer card payment or who are booking through a website checkout. Stripe has the cleaner professional interface and integrates well with scheduling tools like Acuity. PayPal is more familiar to some older clients.

Collect payment before or at the start of the session. A clear cancellation policy (twenty-four hour notice for a refund, or a rebooking credit) protects your time.

## Privacy and PIPEDA Compliance

As a Canadian business handling personal information, you are subject to PIPEDA (the Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act). PIPEDA governs how you collect, use, and store personal information about your clients.

Key obligations under PIPEDA include:

- Having a clear privacy policy that explains what information you collect and how it is used.
- Obtaining consent before collecting personal information.
- Storing client information securely and limiting access to it.
- Providing clients with access to their information if requested.
- Notifying affected individuals and the Privacy Commissioner of Canada in the event of a data breach that creates a real risk of significant harm.

For a solo pet bereavement practitioner, compliance is straightforward. Use a reputable cloud storage provider (Google Drive, OneDrive, or Dropbox with a paid plan) to store session notes securely. Do not store session recordings on public or shared servers. Use Zoom's waiting room and passcode features. Have a one-page privacy policy on your website.

Pet bereavement support is not a health service under PIPEDA's more stringent health information provisions. That said, the information you hold about clients is sensitive and should be treated accordingly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need to record sessions?**
No. Recording sessions is not necessary for TRACE practice and introduces complexity around consent and storage. Most practitioners do not record sessions.

**What if a client becomes distressed and I am concerned for their safety?**
The TRACE framework is a structured grief support tool, not a clinical mental health intervention. If a client presents with acute mental health concerns (such as suicidal ideation), your obligation is to refer them to an appropriate professional (BC Crisis Line: 1-800-784-2433, or 310Mental Health: 310-6789). Include this information in your intake paperwork.

**Can I work with clients outside BC?**
Yes. Online delivery means you can work with clients anywhere in Canada or internationally. Be aware that different privacy laws may apply for clients in other provinces or countries. For Canadian clients outside BC, PIPEDA remains your primary framework.

**What is the best time of day for online sessions?**
Many pet bereavement clients prefer daytime or early evening slots. Avoid very late evening sessions if you can: the emotional weight of this work is significant and you need time to decompress after sessions.
