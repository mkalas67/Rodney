# How to Start a Pet Loss Support Business in Chicago

Chicago is a city of neighborhoods, and in most of them, dogs are a daily presence on the sidewalks, in the parks, and in the coffee shops. From Lincoln Park to Wicker Park to Hyde Park, Chicagoans are deeply attached to their animals. When a pet dies, the loss hits hard, and in a city with a strong but practical workforce culture, many people have no idea where to turn for structured support. That is a gap a trained practitioner can fill.

In a city of nearly 2.7 million people, that represents an enormous number of animal companions and, inevitably, an enormous number of people navigating loss each year. Chicago's pet ownership culture has particular characteristics worth understanding. Chicago winters are genuinely isolating for many residents, and a companion animal often becomes the anchor of routine and comfort during those months. Losing that animal in winter can be especially acute.

The city also has a substantial population of veterinary and animal care professionals, groomers, and shelter workers who witness pet grief regularly and often feel helpless to direct clients anywhere useful. Building relationships within this community is both an ethical service and a practical business strategy.

Chicago's broader workforce is practical and outcomes-focused. Clients here tend to respond well to structured, professional support with a clear framework, rather than open-ended and undefined "emotional support." The TRACE model, with its defined five-step process, is well suited to this market.

## What Does a Pet Loss Practitioner Do?

A pet loss practitioner provides structured support to people grieving the death of a companion animal. Sessions take place in person or via video call. The work is not clinical therapy. You are not diagnosing a mental health condition or providing psychotherapy. Instead, you facilitate a series of structured conversations that help clients move through their grief with purpose and intention.

A framework like TRACE, which stands for Therapeutic Remembrance for Animal Companions and their Endings, provides the structure. The five steps are: Tell the Story, Recognize the Bond, Acknowledge the Pain, Celebrate the Life, and Embrace What Remains. Most practitioners work with a client over four to six sessions. Some offer single focused memorial sessions, while others build longer packages for more complex grief presentations.

Memorial pages, which TRACE practitioners receive as part of their training, give clients a lasting record of their animal's life and are a valued add-on service.

## Do You Need a License or Certification to Offer Pet Loss Support in Chicago?

Illinois does not require a clinical license to offer pet loss support. The licensing requirements that apply to licensed clinical social workers, licensed professional counselors, and psychologists in Illinois relate to the diagnosis and treatment of mental health conditions. Pet loss support does not involve either.

The distinction in how you describe your services is important. You provide structured grief support and compassionate guidance. You do not provide therapy, make diagnoses, or treat mental health conditions. Within that framing, you can operate legally in Illinois without a clinical license.

Professional credentials still matter. Chicago's veterinary and animal care community, and clients themselves, expect a trained practitioner to carry a recognized certification. If you hold existing qualifications in counseling, social work, nursing, coaching, or related fields, those credentials are complementary. A specialist pet loss certification is the core credential you need to operate with authority.

### What if I work in a veterinary practice and want to add this to my role?
Many vet techs and vet nurses in Chicago are the first people a client speaks to after a pet dies. TRACE certification gives you the structured skills to have those conversations with confidence. Some practices support staff who hold specialist certifications, and a few may even fund training for the right employee.

### Is there a difference between pet loss support and pet grief therapy?
Yes, and the distinction matters legally and practically. Pet grief therapy implies a clinical intervention. Pet loss support and pet bereavement counseling describe what you actually do: structured, evidence-informed compassionate support outside a clinical framework. Using accurate language protects both you and your clients.

### How do I handle a client in acute distress or mental health crisis?
Your training will cover this. In summary, a pet loss practitioner is not equipped to manage mental health crises, and you should have a clear protocol for referring clients who present with crisis-level distress to a licensed mental health professional. The TRACE framework assumes clients who are grieving, not clients in psychiatric crisis.

### Can I offer group sessions in Chicago?
Group pet loss support sessions are a legitimate and growing offering. Community workshops, group memorial sessions, and online grief circles are all possible formats. The Extended TRACE programme's Widening the Circle module addresses working with groups and families, making it a natural fit for practitioners who want to build this dimension into their practice.
