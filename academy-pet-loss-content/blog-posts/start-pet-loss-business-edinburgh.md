# How to Start a Pet Loss Support Business in Edinburgh

Edinburgh has a character unlike any other city in Britain. Its residents are known for quiet loyalty, a strong sense of community, and an attachment to place and tradition that extends naturally to the animals who share their lives. Whether it is a collie walked along the Pentland Hills, a cat curled on the windowsill of a New Town tenement, or a rescue dog that became a family's anchor through difficult years, pets in Edinburgh are taken seriously. And when they are gone, the grief is taken seriously too. What is missing is enough professional support for people to turn to when that grief arrives.

## The Growing Demand for Pet Loss Support in Edinburgh

Scotland sits within the UK average of approximately 51% of households owning a pet, and Edinburgh's blend of urban and semi-rural communities generates high rates of dog and cat ownership across the city. The green spaces that make Edinburgh distinctive, Holyrood Park, the Meadows, Corstorphine Hill, the Water of Leith walkway, are a constant presence in dog owners' lives, and the social fabric woven through daily walks and shared outdoor spaces means the loss of a dog has particular weight here.

Edinburgh has a relatively well-developed professional services sector and a population that is comfortable engaging with therapy, coaching, and wellness support. Human grief counselling services exist through charities, NHS mental health pathways, and the city's private practice community. Pet loss support, however, is not part of that landscape in any systematic way. The Royal Dick School of Veterinary Studies at the University of Edinburgh makes the city a significant centre of veterinary medicine, yet the human grief that follows animal death remains largely unsupported as a professional category.

That gap is your opportunity. A qualified pet loss practitioner in Edinburgh operates in an uncrowded space, supported by a city that already has strong referral infrastructure and a population willing to invest in professional support.

## What Does a Pet Loss Practitioner Do?

A pet loss practitioner provides structured, one-to-one support to people grieving the death of a companion animal. Sessions run for 50 to 60 minutes and take place in person or via video call.

The work is guided conversation rather than clinical treatment. Using a clear, repeatable framework, you help clients move through their grief step by step: telling the story of their animal, recognising the full depth and meaning of the bond, acknowledging the pain of the loss without shortcutting it, celebrating the animal's life and what it brought to their world, and beginning to find a way to carry that relationship forward in memory rather than physical presence.

This is not a role that requires clinical mental health qualifications. Approximately 97% of what a skilled pet loss practitioner does is compassionate, structured conversation, work that is fully accessible to people from veterinary nursing, life coaching, counselling, social work, education, or personal experience. The discipline comes from the framework. The impact comes from the combination of structure and genuine human care.

## Do You Need a Formal Qualification to Practise in Edinburgh?

In Scotland, as across the UK, there is no statutory regulation that applies specifically to pet loss practitioners. The role is not governed by a mandatory licensing body, and there is no legal requirement to hold a specific qualification before offering pet bereavement support.

In practice, Edinburgh's professional culture makes certification important. The city has a highly educated population that is accustomed to assessing credentials before engaging service providers. Veterinary practices in the city, many of them connected to or influenced by the broader veterinary medicine community centred on the Royal (Dick) School of Veterinary Studies, will take you more seriously as a referral partner if you present a credible, named qualification.

Pet insurance providers, animal welfare organisations, and Edinburgh's wellness community all make the same assessment. A recognised certificate from a structured programme signals seriousness and accountability. It is also the basis on which you can approach referral partners with confidence rather than apology.

Existing qualifications in counselling, veterinary nursing, social work, coaching, or education are useful complements to a pet loss certificate, but none of them is a prerequisite.

## Building Your Client Base in Edinburgh

Edinburgh's compact, well-connected character works in your favour when building a practice. The city is small enough that a reputation travels, and professional communities in the veterinary and wellness sectors are closely networked.

Veterinary practices are the single most important referral relationship to establish. Edinburgh has a strong concentration of practices, from independent clinics in Stockbridge, Bruntsfield, and Corstorphine to larger group practices in the south of the city. Given Edinburgh's connection to veterinary medicine through the university, many local practices have staff who are particularly thoughtful about the human dimension of animal loss. Introduce yourself professionally with your TRACE certificate and ask whether they have a protocol for signposting bereaved clients.

Providers operating in the Lothians region meet families at the most acute moment of grief and are natural partners for a qualified practitioner. Online visibility in Edinburgh's well-connected pet owner community is readily achievable. ## Key Referral Partners to Approach in Edinburgh

### Veterinary practices
Edinburgh has a higher than average concentration of veterinary expertise, with practices across every part of the city and a population attuned to professional animal care. A well-written introduction accompanied by your TRACE certificate and a brief explanation of what you offer is the right first contact. Practices in dog-walking neighbourhoods such as Newington, Marchmont, and Trinity are worth prioritising.

### Pet cremation and memorial services
Providers in the Lothians region work with grieving families directly and consistently. A relationship with one or two cremation services can become a long-term referral source. Most providers will welcome having a qualified practitioner they can recommend to families who are clearly struggling.

### Pet insurance providers
Major providers serving Scottish customers include Petplan and ManyPets. Exploring whether they have a bereavement support section or a partner directory that includes pet loss practitioners is a worthwhile step.

### Animal rescue centres
The Edinburgh Dog and Cat Home and other rescue organisations in the city are closely connected to the animal-owning community. They occasionally work with families following the death of a recently rehomed animal and may welcome a named contact for professional referrals.

### Local pet businesses
Groomers, boarding kennels, and dog walkers who are well established in Edinburgh's pet community are trusted by their clients. A short conversation, a business card, and a clear explanation of your service is a low-cost way to extend your professional network.

## What to Charge for Pet Loss Support Sessions

Pet loss practitioners in Edinburgh typically charge between £50 and £80 per session at the start of their practice. As experience and reputation develop, rates of £75 to £100 are realistic and consistent with what Edinburgh clients pay for specialist wellness and therapeutic support more broadly.

A free initial call of around 20 minutes is standard and helps convert enquiries into paying clients. It gives people the chance to experience your approach before committing, and it reduces the hesitation that many people feel about seeking professional grief support.

Memorial pages are priced at your discretion. Including one with a session package adds perceived value without requiring significant time. Standalone memorial pages at £30 to £55 are a reasonable benchmark.

At £70 per session, four clients per week generates £280 per week. At that rate, your TRACE certification cost is recovered within five weeks of consistent practice.

No. Pet loss support is not regulated in the same way as clinical counselling or psychotherapy in the UK. A recognised certification such as TRACE is essential for building professional credibility, but it does not require a prior counselling qualification. Many effective practitioners come from veterinary nursing, coaching, or personal experience with animal loss.
