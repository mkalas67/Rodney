## What Does a Pet Loss Practitioner Do?

A pet loss practitioner supports people through the grief of losing a companion animal, using structured, compassionate conversations. Sessions are typically conducted in person or via video, and video sessions are particularly practical in a city that is actively expanding and where traffic can be unpredictable.

The work is not clinical therapy. You do not diagnose or treat mental health conditions. Instead, you guide clients through a defined process that helps them tell the story of their animal, name the bond, process the pain, honor the life, and carry the loss forward in a healthy way. The TRACE framework, which stands for Therapeutic Remembrance for Animal Companions and their Endings, provides a five-step method: Tell the Story, Recognize the Bond, Acknowledge the Pain, Celebrate the Life, and Embrace What Remains.

Most practitioners see clients over four to six sessions. Some offer single memorial sessions for clients who want a focused, one-time experience. Memorial pages, which TRACE practitioners receive on completion of the programme, are a valued additional offering that clients often appreciate as part of the overall support experience.

## Do You Need a License or Certification to Offer Pet Loss Support in Austin?

Texas does not require a clinical license to offer pet loss support. The licensing requirements for licensed professional counselors and licensed clinical social workers in Texas apply to the diagnosis and treatment of mental health conditions. Pet loss support, when accurately described, falls outside that regulatory scope.

The language you use matters. You provide structured grief support and compassionate guidance. You do not provide therapy, diagnose conditions, or treat mental illness. Within that framing, you can operate legally in Texas without a clinical license.

That said, professional credentials are important in Austin's market. The city's educated and discerning population will look for evidence of training and recognized credentials before engaging with any wellness practitioner. A specialist certification from a programme built on established grief theory is the professional baseline. Existing qualifications in coaching, counseling, nursing, or social work are complementary assets, but neither is a prerequisite for the other.
